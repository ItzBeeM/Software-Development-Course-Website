Lesson 5: Choosing the Best Development Methods and the Development Cycle as a Whole.

This lessson will cover the various software development methodologies, such as Agile, Scrum, and Waterfall, and discuss their advantages and disadvantages. And talk how to choose the best one for a given project. We will also provide an overview of the software development lifecycle.

The steps of the software development lifecycle typically include requirements gathering, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Requirements gathering involves understanding the needs and goals of the project, and defining the features and functionality that the software should have. In the design phase you create a blueprint for the software, including the architecture, user interface, and database design. Implementation is where you actually write the code. Testing involves verifying that the software works as intended and meets the requirements. Sometimes it's done by specific people called testers and sometimes by automatic tests, but in smaller projects it's often done by the developers themselves. Deployment is the process of releasing the software to users, and maintenance involves ongoing support and updates to ensure that the software continues to function properly and meet the needs of users.

It's critically important to choose the right development method for your project. There are many different methodologies, Let's start with the one that to some might seem as the best and most efficient, but in reality in most cases isn't, the waterfall method. The work in this one is divided into sequential phases, such as requirements gathering, design, implementation, testing, and deployment. Each phase must be completed before the next one begins. The problem with this method is that it's inflexible, if the requirements change during the development process, it can be difficult and costly to go back and make changes to the previous phases. It may seem that this method prevents chaos and keeps everything organized, but in reality it can lead to a lot of wasted time and resources.

On the other hand we have the agile method, which is a more flexible and iterative approach to software development. The main idea behind agile is to break the project into smaller, manageable chunks called sprints, and to deliver working software at the end of each sprint. So instead of trying to create the whole product from the start you create working portions of it. You can show them to client and easily change stuff that they doesn't like or that you realize doesn't work as well as you thought. This method allows for more adaptability and responsiveness to change, but it can also lead to a lack of structure and organization if not implemented properly. There are also so called frameworks in agile, such as scrum, which provide a more structured approach to implementing agile principles. Scrum involves specific roles, such as the product owner, scrum master, and development team, and specific ceremonies, such as sprint planning, daily stand-ups, sprint reviews, and retrospectives.

There is also the kanban method, which is a visual approach to managing work, where tasks are represented on a board and moved through different stages of completion, like to do, in progress, and done. It's also important to limit the amount of work in progress to ensure that the team is focused and not overwhelmed with too many tasks at once. This framework has a continuous flow of work, unlike scrum which has specific sprints. The third one is Extreme Programming (XP), which emphasizes the quality of code. The developers work in pairs, there is a lot of testing. It also delivers working software in short cycles, often weekly. And customer representative is present at site to provide feedback and clarify requirements.

There are many other methodologies and frameworks, such as Lean, DevOps, etc., and it's important to choose the one that best fits the needs of your project and your team. It's also worth noting that many teams use a hybrid approach, combining elements of different methodologies to create a process that works best for them.
